Land Cover Classification

The land use land cover (LULC) map is extensively employed for different purposes. Machine learning (ML) algorithms applied in remote sensing (RS) data have been proven effective in image classification, object detection, and semantic segmentation. Previous studies have shown that random forest (RF) and support vector machine (SVM) consistently achieve high accuracy for land classification. Considering the important role of Portugal’s Serra da Estrela Natural Park (PNSE) in biodiversity and nature conversation at an international scale, the availability of timely data on the PNSE for emergency evaluation and periodic assessment is crucial. In this study, the application of RF and SVM classifiers, and object-based (OBIA) and pixel-based (PBIA) approaches, with Sentinel-2A imagery was evaluated using Google Earth Engine (GEE) platform for the land cover classification of a burnt area in the PNSE. This aimed to detect the land cover change and closely observe the burnt area and vegetation recovery after the 2022 wildfire. The combination of RF and OBIA achieved the highest accuracy in all evaluation metrics. At the same time, a comparison with the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) map and Conjunctural Land Occupation Map (COSc) of 2023 year indicated that the SVM and PBIA map resembled the maps better.

Land Cover Change Detection

Post-wildfire land cover change affects the ecosystem dynamics, biodiversity system, and ecological processes. While land cover plays a key role in environmental conditions in multiple ways, detecting the land cover change during a wildfire is critical. It enables the authorities to develop prompt disaster management strategies to prevent fatalities caused by landslides and flash floods following a wildfire. Furthermore, periodic assessments scrutinizing burnt area recovery and vegetation dynamics after a wildfire provide valuable information for land management and biodiversity recovery monitoring.


The land cover maps generated by the models were exported to QGIS software for further analysis. The land cover maps were first clipped with the major burnt area extent. Descriptive statistics of NDVI and NBR were computed. The dNBR map was used to analyze the rationality of post-wildfire land cover change relative to fire severity. Land cover maps of the study area's pre-wildfire 2022, post-wildfire 2022, and summer 2023 were intersected to detect the land cover change.

100-Year Flood Analysis

This study simulates flood-prone areas in São Paulo’s Aricanduva basin, modeling potential flooding for a 100-year return period. Using historical rainfall data and a calculated time of concentration of 10.26 hours, we identified significant flood-prone zones, influenced by low basin elevations and extensive urbanization, which has drastically reduced infiltration and increased runoff potential by over 18,000%. Our findings align closely with São Paulo’s official drainage studies, highlighting similar problem areas and validating our approach as a reliable diagnostic for preliminary flood risk assessment. The study’s limitations, such as the exclusion of existing drainage infrastructure and more recent climate data, suggest areas for future refinement in urban flood management planning.


Developed by Ashwin Kumar, Fernanda Costa Cavallaro, and Tan Yan Cheng

Network Analysis

This map analyzes emergency response accessibility from fire stations in Cascais using network analysis to evaluate service areas within 5, 10, 15, and 20 minutes. It shows that many areas are accessible within 0 to 5 minutes, and all fire stations can reach the Sintra-Cascais Natural Park within 15 minutes for an initial attack. However, access to forested areas presents challenges for firefighting efforts, as indicated on the map.

Nature-Based Solutions

This project proposes nature-based solutions for the urbanized Fontainhas region in Porto, originally a rural area that became densely populated with worker housing in the 18th-19th centuries. Due to urban expansion and high-density development, Fontainhas now faces environmental challenges such as poor infrastructure, flooding, and inadequate green spaces. Key proposals include (a) Terraced Flood Management, (b) Building Renovations, (c) Community Spaces in Vacant Areas, (d) Green Parking and Rain Gardens, and (e) Enhanced Public Squares and Green Corridors, to improve local resilience, support climate adaptation, and foster community well-being by integrating green infrastructure throughout Fontainhas.


Developed by Bruna Gazzoni, Fernanda Cavallaro, Tan Yan Cheng, and Victória Rampazzo

Explainable AI (XAI)

AI-driven models are increasingly common in research, delivering impressive prediction accuracies. While we carefully select algorithms, tune parameters, and validate results, how often do we truly understand what drives our model's decisions?



This is where Explainable AI (XAI) becomes crucial. XAI methods help us peek inside the "black box" of complex models, making their decision-making process transparent and interpretable, especially important when presenting results to non-technical stakeholders and policymakers who need to trust and act on these predictions.
